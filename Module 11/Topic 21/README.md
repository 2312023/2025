1. A **Stop-limit order** combines the features of a **stop-loss order** and a **limit order**.
2. It triggers a **limit order** when the stock reaches a specified stop price.
3. Unlike a standard stop-loss, execution is not guaranteed because it becomes a limit order.
4. Traders use stop-limit orders to **control the price at which a trade executes**.
5. A buy stop-limit order triggers a **limit buy order** once the price reaches or exceeds the stop price.
6. A sell stop-limit order triggers a **limit sell order** once the price falls to the stop price.
7. Stop-limit orders are ideal for **volatile markets** where prices can swing sharply.
8. Traders use this order type to **enter or exit positions with precision**.
9. The **stop price** determines when the order is activated.
10. The **limit price** defines the maximum or minimum price at which execution can occur.
11. If the stock price moves beyond the limit price after the stop is triggered, the order may **remain unfilled**.
12. Stop-limit orders prevent **slippage** that occurs with market stop-loss orders.
13. They are widely used by **swing traders, positional traders, and intraday traders**.
14. Traders often set the stop slightly **above or below technical levels** to avoid premature triggering.
15. Limit prices are strategically set to **maximize execution probability**.
16. Stop-limit orders require **continuous monitoring** if placed for volatile securities.
17. They are crucial in situations where traders want to **exit during a price drop but avoid market order risks**.
18. Stop-limit can be used for both **entry and exit strategies**.
19. They are often combined with **support/resistance analysis** to optimize execution.
20. Brokers provide various validity options such as **Day, GTC, or GTD** for stop-limit orders.
21. Partial fills are possible if the available quantity at the limit price is less than the order size.
22. Stop-limit orders are commonly used during **earnings announcements or news events**.
23. They help traders **control trade execution** while minimizing market impact.
24. Traders must understand **market liquidity**, as thinly traded stocks may result in non-execution.
25. Stop-limit orders protect profits by **locking in a minimum acceptable price**.
26. They reduce the need for constant **manual intervention** during price swings.
27. Stop-limit orders contribute to **discipline and risk management** in trading.
28. They are suitable for **leveraged trades**, where uncontrolled slippage can cause significant losses.

30. Investors often use stop-limit for **gap-up or gap-down scenarios** to control entry and exit.
